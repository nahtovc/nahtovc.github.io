Whole Plant Foods and Fasting

From two decades of working in the medical industry, I understand how pharmaceutical drugs and medical procedures can be lifesaving in emergency care. As described below, some physicians have found there are more effective ways to treat chronic diseases such as hypertension and type 2 diabetes. We have tremendous power over our health through nutrition.

The Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine (founded by Dr. Neal D. Barnard in 1985) and Preventive Medicine Research Institute (founded by Dr. Dean Ornish in 1984) are nonprofit research and advocacy organizations for whole-food, plant-based nutrition. Their many studies, in collaboration with scientists and medical institutions, show that lifestyle change including a diet of whole plant foods can (1) improve digestion and immune system health; (2) stabilize blood sugar and benefit weight loss; (3) reduce high blood pressure, cholesterol, and inflammation; and (4) prevent and reverse the progression of chronic diseases such as heart disease and type 2 diabetes.

In the 82-minute video and book "How Not to Die" (Flatiron 2015), Dr. Michael Greger presents scientific evidence that a diet of whole plant foods is protective against 15 top causes of premature death, and allows people to live healthier lives. His Daily Dozen is intended as a guide of the healthiest foods to include every day.

Studies show that consuming a majority of calories early in the day is better for metabolic health and weight loss [1 2]. Eating can be limited to an 8-hour time period or less, and fasting done through the remainder of the day.

Another method is to alternate between a day of consuming no calories, and a day of eating. Dr. Jason Fung has seen in patients that alternate-day zero-calorie fasting resulted in improved weight, blood pressure, cholesterol, triglycerides, glucose, and insulin, along with reduced medication dependency and reversal of type 2 diabetes. In an article and 49-minute video, he explains how fasting changes hormone levels to maintain energy by burning stored fat, whereas frequent calorie-restricted eating reduces basal metabolic rate and increases hunger.

Excerpt from James DiNicolantonio, PharmD., Jason Fung, MD. The Longevity Solution (1st ed., p. 41). Victory Belt 2019.

Because hunger is such a fundamental instinct, it’s virtually impossible to ignore hunger over the long term; hunger dooms many weight-loss programs to failure. Fasting, on the other hand, often paradoxically decreases food cravings and hunger. Many patients note decreased hunger when using intermittent fasting for weight loss. They often comment that they think their stomach shrank when, in reality, hunger signaling decreased.

In a study published in 2016, a randomized controlled trial of 25 obese adults compared a diet of daily calorie restriction (with frequent eating) versus alternate-day zero-calorie fasting [3]. After 32 weeks, the alternate-day fasting group had lost twice as much visceral fat (inside the abdomen), and significantly better maintained their overall fat loss and lean mass. The alternate-day fasting group also had a 23% increase in brain-derived neurotrophic factor (associated with enhanced cognitive function), whereas the daily calorie-restricted group had a 19% decrease.

As detailed in "The Alternate-Day Diet" (James B. Johnson, MD., Donald R. Laub Sr., MD. Penguin 2013), animal studies find that alternate-day zero-calorie fasting positively influences health and longevity to a greater degree than daily calorie restriction. While fasting, cellular repair increases and gene expression changes for disease prevention. Another benefit is a euphoric, energized state of mind. The authors and colleagues have seen in patients that alternate-day fasting ameliorated asthma, autoimmune disease (rheumatoid arthritis), cardiac arrhythmias, infectious diseases, insulin resistance, Meniere’s disease, menopause-related hot flashes, osteoarthritis, seasonal allergies, and Tourette’s syndrome.

In the books "Fasting Can Save Your Life" (NHA 2009) and "The Science And Fine Art of Fasting" (Mockingbird 2019), Dr. Herbert M. Shelton relates his extensive knowledge. From 1928 to 1981 over 400,000 patients were treated at Dr. Shelton’s Health School, which included therapeutic fasting and whole plant foods. Dr. Shelton personally supervised over 30,000 fasts. Patients had improvement in cognition, digestion, energy, sensory organs, and various conditions such as arthritis, asthma, benign tumor, colitis, eczema, enlarged prostate, gallstones, hay fever, heart disease, hypertension, impotence, migraine, multiple sclerosis, nephritis, Parkinson’s disease, peptic ulcer, psoriasis, sterility, and type 2 diabetes.

In the books "The Pleasure Trap" (Douglas J. Lisle, PhD., Alan Goldhamer, DC. Healthy Living 2006) and "Can Fasting Save Your Life?" (Toshia Myers, PhD., Alan Goldhamer, DC. BPC 2024), the authors discuss how fasting and a diet of whole plant foods can overcome dietary excesses and promote health. The authors are affiliated with TrueNorth Health Center, which since 1984 has medically supervised fasting for over 25,000 patients. Patients are instructed to eat only fruits and vegetables (raw or cooked) during the two days prior to a water-only fast lasting typically 1–3 weeks, and afterward follow a refeed as described below. With appropriate diet and exercise, muscle mass can be quickly regained and fat loss maintained long term. Patients have successfully recovered from arthritis, asthma, autoimmune diseases, brain trauma, chemical toxicity, fibroid tumors, heart disease, hypertension, long COVID, lymphoma, obesity, skin lesion, type 2 diabetes, and many other conditions.

TrueNorth Health Foundation conducts related research and education. In the following study, 174 hypertensive patients fasted for 10–11 days on average, followed by 6–7 days of transitioning to a diet of whole plant foods low in fat and sodium. The average reduction in blood pressure was 37/13 mm Hg — far more effective than the 12/6 mm Hg average reduction for standard oral antihypertensive drugs with side effects.

Excerpt from Alan Goldhamer, DC., Douglas J. Lisle, PhD., et al. Medically Supervised Water-only Fasting in the Treatment of Hypertension. J Manip Physiol Ther 2001;24(9):336–337.

The water-only fasting period was followed by a period of supervised refeeding initiated by the consumption of juices made from fresh raw fruits and vegetables. Patients received 12 ounces of fresh juice every 3 hours during the juice phase (approximately 1 day of juices only for each week of water-only fasting). The juice phase was then followed by a diet of fresh raw fruits and vegetables (approximately 1 day for each week of water-only fasting). Subsequent to these transitional regimens, a diet of whole natural foods was introduced.

During water-only fasting it is important to monitor for electrolyte imbalance. Symptoms include arrhythmia (irregular or fast heart rate), confusion, dizziness, fatigue, headache, irritability, muscle cramps, muscle spasms or weakness, nausea and vomiting, and numbness or tingling in extremities [4]. A significant electrolyte imbalance can cause coma, seizure, or sudden cardiac arrest. When fasting for a week or longer, afterward there can be serious complications if food is introduced too quickly [5].

Macronutrients

Carbohydrates consumed beyond the body's immediate needs are used to build glycogen (stored energy in the liver and muscles) and burned as heat. Studies show that carbohydrates are not normally stored as body fat [1 2 3], unless repeatedly overconsumed [4]. Foods high in complex carbohydrates such as potato and rice are sometimes mistakenly blamed for weight problems, because they are commonly eaten together with high-fat foods readily stored as body fat.

A diet based primarily on vegetables, whole grains, and legumes can provide 10% (or more) of total calories from protein, which is sufficient for children and adults per the U.S. National Academy of Medicine (2005 AMDRs) [5]. In a 2003 article Dr. John McDougall discusses studies that show it is not necessary to combine different plant foods to create an amino acid pattern resembling that found in animal proteins [6].

Multigrain Gluten-Free Flatbread

Ingredients

I use various grains such as amaranth, buckwheat, non-GMO corn, millet, quinoa, and sorghum. I grind these a half pound at a time in a large powerful Vitamix blender, mix together into multigrain flour, and store extra flour in the freezer.

Citric acid and potassium bicarbonate act as a leavening agent, creating carbon dioxide gas bubbles which cause the bread to expand and become fluffy. The reaction also creates potassium citrate, a common natural preservative.

Guar gum powder is a natural ingredient derived from guar beans. It is used in gluten-free baked goods as a binder, thickener, and stabilizer for better rise, elastic structure, and moisture content.

Circular Loaf 1" Thick, 12" Wide

Add 1.5 lb of flour into a mixing bowl. Sprinkle with 1 US teaspoon of guar gum powder and 0.5 US teaspoon of potassium bicarbonate crystalline powder. Stir to mix evenly.

In a separate container, add 2.5 US cup of water and 0.5 US teaspoon of citric acid crystalline powder. Stir until dissolved. Pour liquid into mixing bowl that contains flour. Stir to form a batter.

Sprinkle flour onto bakeware (e.g. 13.5" Cuisinel cast-iron round griddle) to prevent sticking. Parchment paper is another option. Scoop batter onto bakeware, sprinkle with flour, and flatten with hands.

Bake in 350 °F (177 °C) oven for 40 minutes, or until center of bread is firm. Transfer bread to a wire rack for cooling.

If refridgerated the bread becomes more firm due to starch recrystallization. Reheating can briefly make it softer.

Circular Loaf 2" Thick, 12" Wide

Double each of the ingredients listed above. Increase baking time by 10 minutes.

Rice

In research by scientists Andrew Meharg and John Duxbury, basmati rice (grown in India and Pakistan) and jasmine rice (grown in Thailand) were found to contain the least amount of toxic inorganic arsenic [1]. Consumer Reports had similar findings, with organic basmati rice (grown in California) also testing among the lowest for arsenic.

Andrew Meharg found the following method to reduce arsenic content of rice by 82% [2].

  1. Soak rice in water overnight. This opens the grain’s structure and allows water-soluble arsenic within to release.
  2. Drain rice in the morning and rinse with clean water.
  3. For every one part of rice, add five parts of water to a cooking pot. Cook rice until tender. Avoid cooking until dry since released arsenic will be reabsorbed.
  4. Drain excess water from rice and rinse with clean hot water.

For whole-grain rice, I use the above method with the first step slightly modified. For each 1 US cup of dried rice, add 1 US teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) and 3 US cup of water to a stainless-steel stockpot. Stir until dissolved. In this step, very little sodium is absorbed into the rice. Allow to soak overnight, then drain and rinse well. The rice will require significantly less cooking time. A potential additional benefit is removal of pesticides if present [3].

Legumes

A staple food in many cultures, legumes (beans, chickpeas, lentils, peas) are high in protein, fiber, and polyphenols with anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and neuroprotective effects. Studies indicate that legumes can (1) benefit weight control; (2) improve blood pressure, blood glucose levels, cholesterol, and triglycerides; and (3) reduce risk of cancer, heart disease, and type 2 diabetes [1 2].

In the book "The Science of Good Cooking" (1st ed., pp. 1246–1262. Cook’s Illustrated 2012), the editors at America’s Test Kitchen describe how soaking dried legumes in water with sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) will make them softer, easier to digest, and require significantly less cooking time. Very little sodium is absorbed into the legumes. A potential additional benefit is removal of pesticides if present [3].

Soak and Bake Method

  1. For each 1 US cup of dried legumes, add 1 US teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate and 3 US cup of water to a stainless-steel stockpot or bowl. Stir until dissolved. Do one of the following (not both):
    • Quick soak: Heat legumes in stockpot on stove until boiling, then turn off heat and allow to soak covered with lid for one hour (42.5% reduction in gas-forming compounds per America’s Test Kitchen).
    • Slow soak: Allow legumes to soak at room temperature for 8–24 hours (28% reduction in gas-forming compounds per America’s Test Kitchen).
  2. Drain legumes and rinse well, then add to a cast-iron Dutch oven (e.g. Amazon Basics 7-quart) which provides consistent heat to evenly cook. Add fresh water as desired.
  3. With lid in place, bake in 350 °F (177 °C) oven until legumes are almost tender (center of legumes may still be firm). Baking time will depend on amount and type of legumes, typically 30–60 minutes.

For convenience I prepare a large amount and store in "7-cup round glass food storage containers." I put one container in the refridgerator to eat baked legumes for multiple meals. I put the other containers in the freezer, and as needed transfer a container to the refridgerator.

For a quick way to make bean dip or soup, I puree baked legumes in a blender. This can make digestion easier by mechanically breaking down the fiber, which acts as a prebiotic for healthy flora.

Nuts and Seeds

Studies link regularly eating nuts and seeds to longer lifespan, and reduced risk of cancer and cardiovascular disease [1 2]. Raw flaxseed and walnuts have among the highest omega-3 ALA and antioxidant levels found in nuts and seeds. Compared to golden flaxseed, brown flaxseed has significantly higher levels [3]. Studies show that flaxseed can benefit weight control, improve blood pressure and cholesterol, and reduce risk of cancer, heart disease, inflammation, stroke, and type 2 diabetes [4 5]. Studies show that walnuts can reduce risk of cancer, heart disease, respiratory disease, and stroke [6].

Clinical research scientist Steve Blake explains in a brief video why he recommends flaxseed (2 tablespoon/day, freshly ground) and walnuts as rich sources of alpha-linolenic acid (ALA), a short-chain omega-3 fatty acid that the body requires in small amount [7]. The body can convert ALA into the long-chain omega-3 fatty acids EPA and DHA. He does not recommend EPA/DHA supplements derived from algae or seafood. EPA is a highly fragile fatty acid that quickly goes rancid, with potential for induced oxidative damage and increased inflammation. One study examined EPA from various sources around the world and found that 91% was already rancid before it entered the supply chain of supplement production and distribution. He mentions that toxic pollutants in seafood are another concern, even after molecular distillation.

Culinary Herbs and Spices

Used to enhance the flavor of food, a culinary herb is the leafy green part of a plant and a spice is any other part. Crushed spices tend to have a stronger aroma and flavor from the essential oils. Both are rich in antioxidants and may have antiviral properties [1 2].

Laboratory analyses found significant levels of toxic heavy metals in commonly sold herbs and spices [3 4]. Some brands (e.g. Frontier Co-Op organic turmeric) test to verify their sources are safe.

Kitchen Items

For liquids and moist foods I avoid aluminum [1 2], cans with chemical lining [3], dishware and enamelware with potential toxic heavy metals [4 5], nonstick coatings, and plastics [6].

I use glass dishes, glass and stainless-steel containers, and stainless-steel utensils. I use cookware made of carbon steel, cast iron, and nickel-free stainless steel.

When I bought cast-iron cookware, initially I used sandpaper to remove the factory seasoning and smooth the cooking surface. To maintain my own natural coating, I apply a thin light coat of flaxseed oil on the cookware and place it upside down in the oven to bake at 450 °F (232 °C) for one hour, with range hood fan on and windows open for ventilation. Afterward I allow the cookware to cool in the closed oven. From prolonged exposure to high heat, flaxseed oil forms a hard polymer that bonds to cast iron and protects from rust.

Boron

Boron is important for vitamin D and parathyroid glands which regulate calcium, magnesium, and phosphorus for healthy bones, joints, and teeth. Supplemental boron may benefit testosterone and estrogen levels, as well as cognition and memory. Boron deficiency can lead to arthritis and calcification of soft tissues. Ascorbic acid powder can be economical for high oral dosage, with no added fillers found in tablets.

Studies find that small amounts of plant pigments (flavonoids) in vitamin C products marketed as "natural" are not significant and not more effective than pure ascorbic acid. Studies find that slow timed-release capsules are not more efficient in increasing vitamin C levels, compared to other oral forms.

Mineral ascorbates are another type of vitamin C. Calcium ascorbate (Ester-C) contains 10% calcium, and sodium ascorbate contains 13% sodium. Excessive amounts of these minerals may not be appropriate when taken orally in high dosage. Mineral ascorbates are less potent as an antioxidant with only one extra electron per molecule to donate.

Ascorbyl palmitate contains 42.5% vitamin C and is not more bioavailable than pure ascorbic acid. When ascorbyl palmitate is taken orally, the small intestine releases the ascorbic acid molecule that is bound to the ester.

Most forms of oral vitamin C require active transport in the bowel for absorption. Liposomal vitamin C is a less common, more expensive form absorbed by a different mechanism in gut cells. A liposome has a spherical phospholipid bilayer, with an internal cavity capable of carrying liquid vitamin C such as ascorbic acid or sodium ascorbate. Absorption can be up to 90% of a high oral dose, depending on how the liposome encapsulation is formulated.

Safety

In this section the term vitamin C refers to ascorbic acid, the type that animals internally synthesize. Vitamin C is a nontoxic, water-soluble substance that does not accumulate in the body. Since the 1940s physicians have safely used high-dose oral vitamin C to treat patients. Potential side effects are temporary gastrointestinal disturbances which quickly subside when dosage is reduced.

There is no compelling, consistent data that indicate any serious adverse effects from high-dose oral vitamin C. No clinical trials have ever shown vitamin C to increase any risk factors in healthy people.

Excerpt from Thomas E. Levy, MD, JD. Schizophrenia Is Chronic Encephalitis. Orthomolecular Medicine News Service 2023.

Vitamin C is the safest of all known nutrient supplements. In fact, there has never been established any dose of vitamin C above which toxicity will reliably ensue. This is consistent with the fact that vitamin C is the molecule on which the physiology of all cells runs, and the healthy function of the body relies on having large amounts of it both inside the cells as well as outside of them. Arguably, vitamin C is the safest consumable agent in existence. Rare individuals can experience minimal side effects, but this should not be confused with any degree of cell-damaging toxicity. By contrast, too much water intake is toxic and can even result in death.

Physicians and researchers experienced with the use of high-dose vitamin C confirm that it does not cause B12 deficiency, birth defect, cataract, DNA damage, infertility, kidney stone, low blood sugar, or miscarriage [37]. The U.S. National Academy of Medicine reports "the in vivo data do not clearly show a causal relationship between excess vitamin C intake" and allergic response, excess iron absorption, kidney stone formation, pro-oxidant effects, reduced vitamin B12 and copper levels, systemic conditioning or rebound scurvy [38].

Observational studies are highly variable in quality of evidence provided, potential bias, and errors. Assuming a cause-and-effect relationship between two variables may be incorrect based on an observed association between them — correlation does not imply causation. For example, people with a medical condition may be found to more frequently take a nutritional supplement to improve their health, and that does not imply that the supplement caused the medical condition.

The article "Cataracts and Vitamins: The Real Story" (Orthomolecular.org 2013) addresses an observational study that found an association between cataract and oral vitamin C, with the incorrect implication that the vitamin C caused the cataract [39]. It is not scientifically plausible. The study contradicts many other studies that show long-term benefit (e.g. 80% reduction in cataract) or short-term no effect.

The article "What Really Causes Kidney Stones (And Why Vitamin C Does Not)" (Orthomolecular.org 2013) addresses an observational study that found an association between kidney stone and oral vitamin C, with the incorrect implication that the vitamin C caused the kidney stone [40]. Professor Emanuel Cheraskin states, "Vitamin C in the urine tends to bind calcium and decrease its free form. This means less chance of calcium’s separating out as calcium oxalate (stones)." Important factors in development of kidney stone are dehydration and high dietary intake of oxalate sources such as spinach.

The article "Kidney stones caused by vitamin C?" (PatrickHolford.com 2013) describes a study at the Kidney Stone Research Laboratory at University of Cape Town [41 42]. Unlike other erroneous studies, for this study they used collection containers with a preservative to keep urine from changing while being stored before testing. Ten subjects took 4 g/day oral vitamin C for five days and laboratory urinalysis found no increased risk factors. A world expert in this field of research, Professor Allen Rodgers indicated that kidney stone could not be caused by use of vitamin C.

A 1994 study published in The Journal of Urology has similar findings. The researchers tested subjects with oral dosages of up to 10 g/day vitamin C for several days and conclude, "no genuine increase in urinary oxalate was demonstrable despite a greatly increased ascorbate intake" [43].

The article "Vitamin C Does Not Cause Kidney Stones" (Orthomolecular.org 2005) mentions a study of 45,251 men that found those taking above 1.5 g/day vitamin C had reduced risk of kidney stone [44]. A follow-up study of 85,557 women for 14 years found the risk was the same for people taking under 250 mg/day and those taking 1.5 g/day. The authors of these large studies state that restricting high-dose vitamin C for potential kidney stone is unwarranted.

Beginning in the 1940s, Dr. William J. McCormick and Dr. Frederick R. Klenner reported that long-term use of high-dose oral vitamin C does not cause kidney stone in healthy adults, and can be protective [45].

Laetrile Quality

Laetrile (with a capital "L") is a refined, concentrated form of natural amygdalin first developed by Ernst T. Krebs Jr. in a patented process. Laetrile turns polarized light in a left-handed direction, and is the form successfully used by the physicians and researchers mentioned above.

Some manufacturers produce iso-amygdalin, an artificial form that may be mislabeled as amygdalin or laetrile (with a lower case "l"). This turns polarized light in both left-handed and right-handed directions, is much less effective against cancer, and can cause adverse reactions in patients.
