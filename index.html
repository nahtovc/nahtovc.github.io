Introduction

From two decades of working in the medical industry, I understand how pharmaceutical drugs and medical procedures can be lifesaving in emergency care. As described below, some physicians have found there are more effective ways to treat chronic diseases such as hypertension and type 2 diabetes. We have tremendous power over our health through nutrition.

Fasting Methods

Daily Intermittent Fasting

Studies showed improved weight loss and metabolic health from consuming a majority of calories early in the day. Eating can be limited to an 8-hour time period or less, and fasting done through the remainder of the day.

Alternate-Day Zero-Calorie Fasting

Another effective method is to alternate between a day of consuming no calories, and a day of normal eating.

Dr. Jason Fung has seen in patients that alternate-day zero-calorie fasting resulted in improved weight, blood pressure, cholesterol, triglycerides, glucose, and insulin, along with reduced medication dependency and reversal of type 2 diabetes. In an article and 49-minute video, he explains how fasting changes hormone levels to maintain energy by burning stored fat, whereas frequent calorie-restricted eating reduces basal metabolic rate and increases hunger.

Excerpt from James DiNicolantonio, PharmD., Jason Fung, MD. The Longevity Solution (1st ed., p. 41). Victory Belt 2019.

Because hunger is such a fundamental instinct, it’s virtually impossible to ignore hunger over the long term; hunger dooms many weight-loss programs to failure. Fasting, on the other hand, often paradoxically decreases food cravings and hunger. Many patients note decreased hunger when using intermittent fasting for weight loss. They often comment that they think their stomach shrank when, in reality, hunger signaling decreased.

In a study published in 2016, a randomized controlled trial of 25 obese adults compared a diet of daily calorie restriction (with frequent eating) versus alternate-day zero-calorie fasting. After 32 weeks, the alternate-day fasting group had lost twice as much visceral fat (inside the abdomen), and significantly better maintained their overall fat loss and lean mass. The alternate-day fasting group also had a 23% increase in brain-derived neurotrophic factor (associated with improved cognitive function), whereas the daily calorie-restricted group had a 19% decrease.

As detailed in "The Alternate-Day Diet" (James B. Johnson, MD., Donald R. Laub Sr., MD. Penguin 2013), animal studies found that alternate-day zero-calorie fasting improved health and longevity to a greater degree than daily calorie restriction. While fasting, cellular repair increases and gene expression changes for disease prevention. Another benefit is a euphoric, energized state of mind. The authors and colleagues have seen alternate-day fasting to benefit patients with asthma, autoimmune disease (rheumatoid arthritis), cardiac arrhythmias, infectious diseases, insulin resistance, Meniere’s disease, menopause-related hot flashes, osteoarthritis, seasonal allergies, and Tourette’s syndrome.

Multiple-Day Water-Only Fasting

In the books "The Science And Fine Art of Fasting" (Mockingbird 2019) and "Fasting Can Save Your Life" (NHA 2009), Dr. Herbert M. Shelton relates his 45 years of supervising over 40,000 fasts for self-healing and weight loss. Patients had improvement in cognition, digestion, energy, sensory organs, and various conditions such as arthritis, asthma, benign tumor, colitis, eczema, enlarged prostate, gallstones, hay fever, heart disease, hypertension, impotence, migraine, multiple sclerosis, nephritis, Parkinson’s disease, psoriasis, sterility, and type 2 diabetes.

In 2024 TrueNorth Health Center is the world’s largest facility that specializes in medically supervised water-only fasting and whole plant foods for health recovery, offering on-site services and remote consultation. They have observed patients successfully overcome arthritis, asthma, autoimmune diseases, brain trauma, chemical toxicity, fibroid tumors, heart disease, hypertension, long COVID, lymphoma, obesity, skin lesion, type 2 diabetes, and many other conditions.

Patients are instructed to eat only fruits and vegetables (raw or cooked) during the two days prior to a water-only fast lasting typically 1–3 weeks, and afterward follow a refeed as described below. Muscle is quickly regained after a fast, while fat loss can be maintained long term with appropriate diet.

In the following study, 174 hypertensive patients did water-only fasting and the average reduction in blood pressure was 37/13 mm Hg — far more effective than the 12/6 mm Hg average reduction for standard oral antihypertensive drugs with side effects.

Excerpt from Alan Goldhamer, DC., Douglas J. Lisle, PhD., et al. Medically Supervised Water-only Fasting in the Treatment of Hypertension. J Manip Physiol Ther 2001;24(9):336–337.

The water-only fasting period was followed by a period of supervised refeeding initiated by the consumption of juices made from fresh raw fruits and vegetables. Patients received 12 ounces of fresh juice every 3 hours during the juice phase (approximately 1 day of juices only for each week of water-only fasting). The juice phase was then followed by a diet of fresh raw fruits and vegetables (approximately 1 day for each week of water-only fasting). Subsequent to these transitional regimens, a diet of whole natural foods was introduced.

I typically limit eating to a 6-hour time period and fast through the remainder of the day. While fasting I consume water, noncaloric supplements, and electrolytes for exercise. For me the most noticeable benefits are improved energy, sleep quality, and body composition.

Every 2–3 weeks I do an extended fast for 66–90 hours (2–3 entire days + 2 partial days). Research indicates that fasting longer than 48 hours will reach the highest level of autophagy, the process of cells recycling old and damaged parts to operate more efficiently.

Whole Plant Foods

Studies have shown that a diet of whole plant foods with minimal processing can (1) improve digestion and immune system health; (2) stabilize blood sugar and benefit weight loss; (3) lower blood pressure, cholesterol, and inflammation; (4) reduce risk of arthritis, cancer, cognitive decline, mental illness, obesity, stroke, and type 2 diabetes; and (5) prevent and reverse heart disease.

The Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine (founded by Dr. Neal D. Barnard in 1985) and Preventive Medicine Research Institute (founded by Dr. Dean Ornish in 1984) are nonprofit research and advocacy organizations for whole-food, plant-based nutrition. Their many studies, in collaboration with scientists and medical institutions, have shown that lifestyle change including a diet of whole plant foods can prevent and reverse the progression of chronic diseases such as cancer, heart disease, and type 2 diabetes. They work to change the way chronic diseases are treated by physicians, where practical dietary interventions empower patients to take control of their own health.

In an 82-minute video and book "How Not to Die" (Flatiron 2015), Dr. Michael Greger presents scientific evidence that a diet of whole plant foods is protective against 15 top causes of premature death, and allows people to live healthier lives. The 96-minute film "Forks Over Knives" (2011) examines how common chronic diseases can be prevented — and in many cases reversed — by adopting a diet of whole plant foods as a powerful way to achieve good health.

A variety of whole plant foods can provide enough protein for health. The U.S. National Academy of Medicine (previously Institute of Medicine) published a Daily Value of 50 g/day protein for adults consuming 2000 calories/day. They arrived at this value by doubling the average adult requirement found by research studies to be 0.4 g/day protein per kg of body weight, equivalent to 22–33 g/day protein for adults weighing 120–180 lb (54.5–81.7 kg).

I choose "organic" whole plant foods when available and affordable for potentially lower exposure to pesticides. To remove these from produce, I wash and then soak for 15 minutes in a solution of 1 US teaspoon sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) per 3 US cups water.

Dietary Salt

Sodium

The estimated adult world average for sodium is 3950 mg/day. The American Heart Association and NASEM issued guidelines for healthy adults to limit consumption of sodium to 1500 mg/day. However, doing so may actually increase mortality.

In a 2018 article Dr. Jason Fung notes that consuming enough sodium is vital for maintaining healthy blood pressure and volume. He describes two large studies that found people with a low sodium intake had 15–18% higher mortality, compared to those with the highest sodium intake. Other trials found that among treated hypertensive patients, those who ate a low sodium diet had increased risk of heart attack.

Similar findings came from a pooled analysis of four studies comprising 133,000 people in 49 countries. Regardless of blood pressure, those who consumed less than 3000 mg/day sodium had significantly increased risk of cardiovascular disease and death, compared to people consuming 4000–5000 mg/day sodium. Among those who consumed over 7000 mg/day sodium, there was increased risk of cardiovascular disease and death for people with hypertension, but not for people with healthy blood pressure.

Purified

I prefer dietary salt (sodium chloride) that is mined from ancient underground mineral deposits and purified by the manufacturer. One such product with no additives is Morton Canning & Pickling Salt. A packed leveled 1 US teaspoon contains approximately 3000 mg sodium. I get iodine from a better source than dietary salt.

Microplastics

A 2018 study found that 25 of 28 different commercial sea salts from around the world contained an abundance of microplastics due to polluted seawater. On average, rock salt (mined from underground) had far less. Microplastic particles are linked to adverse effects on health.

Toxic Lead

Despite marketing claims, unpurified gourmet specialty salts do not contain significant amounts of minerals other than sodium chloride. However, some may contain higher amounts of toxic lead than purified salt. For example, a 2020 study found that 29 of 31 different samples of Himalayan pink salt had higher lead levels than purified table salt, on average 2.5 times more. Excluded from the average as an outlier, one sample of Himalayan pink salt had 129.5 times more lead than purified table salt.

Lead is a toxic heavy metal which can accumulate in the body. Elevated lead levels are common and associated with significantly increased risk of disease and mortality. High-dose oral vitamin C and crushed raw garlic can assist the body in reducing lead levels.

Legumes

A staple food in many cultures, legumes (beans, chickpeas, lentils, peas) are high in protein, fiber, and polyphenols with anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and neuroprotective effects. Studies indicate that legumes can (1) benefit weight control; (2) improve blood pressure, blood glucose levels, cholesterol, and triglycerides; and (3) reduce risk of cancer, heart disease, and type 2 diabetes.

Baked Legumes

Guy Crosby, nutrition professor at Harvard and science editor of America’s Test Kitchen, describes in an article and book "The Science of Good Cooking" (1st ed., p. 1262. Cook’s Illustrated 2012) how a small amount of sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) added to water will make legumes softer, easier to digest, and require significantly less cooking time. (A potential additional benefit is removal of pesticides if present.)

  1. For each 1 US cup of dried legumes, add 1 US teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate and 3 US cup of water to a stainless steel stockpot or bowl. Stir until dissolved. In this step, very little sodium is absorbed into the legumes. Do one of the following (not both):
    • Quick-soak method: Heat legumes in stockpot on stove until boiling, then turn off heat and allow to soak covered with lid for one hour (42.5% reduction in gas-forming compounds).
    • Slow-soak method: Allow legumes to soak at room temperature for 8–24 hours (28% reduction in gas-forming compounds).
  2. Drain legumes and rinse well, then add to a cast-iron Dutch oven (e.g. Amazon Basics 7-quart) which provides consistent heat to evenly cook. Add fresh water to cover legumes. For each 1 US cup of dried legumes used in the previous step, add 0.25 US teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate and stir until dissolved. Add salt and spices as desired. With lid in place, bake in 350 °F (177 °C) oven until legumes are almost tender (center of legumes may still be firm). Baking time will depend on amount and type of legumes, typically 30–60 minutes.

For convenience I prepare a large amount and store in "7-cup round glass food storage containers." I put one container in the refridgerator to eat baked legumes during the week. I put the other containers in the freezer, and remove to thaw in the refridgerator as needed.

Multigrain Gluten-Free Flatbread

Flatbread can be a convenient way to prepare a variety of grain-like foods. My favorites are amaranth, buckwheat, non-GMO corn, quinoa, and sorghum. I grind these a half pound at a time in a large powerful blender (e.g. Vitamix), mix together into multigrain flour, and store in the freezer to keep on hand for use in the following recipe.

In a mixing bowl add 3 US cup of water, 1 US teaspoon of citric acid crystals, and salt as desired. Stir until dissolved. Add packed leveled 5 US cup of multigrain flour, 1 US teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate (baking soda), 1 US teaspoon of guar gum powder, and spices as desired. Stir into batter, adding more flour or water if needed. Sprinkle a thin layer of flour onto griddle (e.g. Cuisinel 13.5" round cast-iron) to prevent sticking. Scoop batter onto griddle, sprinkle with flour, and flatten with hands. Bake in 350 °F (177 °C) oven for 30 minutes.

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Flaxseed

Flaxseed is high in short-chain omega-3 fatty acids, fiber, and antioxidants. Consuming flaxseed may have a mild estrogenic effect, but overall the benefits appear to be significant. Studies indicate flaxseed can benefit weight control, improve blood pressure and cholesterol, and reduce risk of cancer, heart disease, inflammation, stroke, and type 2 diabetes.

I use an electric grinder (KitchenAid blade coffee and spice grinder combo pack) to reduce raw flaxseed into flour, then mix in a bowl with a little water, dried fruit, and spices. Another option is to liquify flaxseed and produce in a large powerful blender (e.g. Vitamix).

Algae Oil

An option for supplementation of long-chain omega-3 fatty acids (preformed EPA and DHA) is algae oil, derived from phytoplankton grown under controlled conditions in fresh water and purified. Phytoplankton are in a different biological kingdom than blue-green algae which may contain toxins.

Fish Oil

Fish oil is often an industry waste product and highly susceptible to becoming rancid, with potential for induced oxidative damage and increased inflammation. Toxic pollutants are another concern, even after molecular distillation.

Rice

Many sources of rice have been found to be high in toxic inorganic arsenic, which is classified as a group-A human carcinogen. Long-term ingestion of arsenic can lead to blocking of blood vessels, heart disease, hypertension, impaired brain function, nerve damage, type 2 diabetes, and various types of cancer.

In studies by scientists Andrew Meharg and John Duxbury, basmati rice (grown in India and Pakistan) and jasmine rice (grown in Thailand) were found to contain the least amount of arsenic. Consumer Reports had similar findings, with organic basmati rice (grown in California) also testing among the lowest for arsenic.

Method by Andrew Meharg found to reduce arsenic content of rice by 82%:

  1. Soak rice in water overnight. This opens the grain’s structure and allows water-soluble arsenic within to release.
  2. Drain rice in the morning and rinse with clean water.
  3. For every one part of rice, add five parts of water to a cooking pot.
  4. Cook rice until tender. Avoid cooking until dry since released arsenic will be reabsorbed.
  5. Drain excess water from rice and rinse with clean hot water.

Sodium Bicarbonate

To prepare whole-grain rice or sorghum, a small amount of sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) added to water will soften the whole grains and require significantly less cooking time on the stove. A potential additional benefit is removal of pesticides if present.

  1. Soaking: For each 1 US cup of dried whole grains, add 1 US teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate and 3 US cup of water to a stainless steel stockpot. Stir until dissolved. In this step, very little sodium is absorbed into the whole grains. Allow to soak overnight, then drain and rinse well.
  2. Cooking: For each 1 US cup of dried whole grains used in the previous step, add 0.25 US teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate to the cooking water and stir until dissolved.

Sauerkraut

Studies found that raw sauerkraut with probiotic bacteria can benefit bones, brain, digestion, heart, immune system, and reduce risk of cancer. Sauerkraut is a potent stimulant for production of stomach acid, which in deficiency is an underlying cause of many intestinal problems.

Sauerkraut is simple to make. Weigh chopped cabbage on food scale. Calculate 2% of cabbage weight (e.g. 1000 g cabbage × 0.02 = 20 g salt) and measure that amount of salt on food scale. Add cabbage and salt to large powerful blender (e.g. Vitamix). Blend to thick consistency, tamping and adding water as needed. Scoop blended mixture to fill glass canning jars. Label with current date, attach lids, and place jars on tray in dark location at 60–75 °F (16–24 °C) for 4–8 weeks, depending on temperature.

Properly fermented sauerkraut can keep for many months when stored at the temperature 35 °F (2 °C) of a typical refrigerator. The benefits of probiotics are lost when heated above 106 °F (41 °C).

Sprouts

Sprouts are highly nutritious and simple to grow at home. Among my favorites are sprouted broccoli, buckwheat, lentils, and sunflower.

I find that a 12-quart stainless steel bain marie with cover (e.g. TigerChef.com Winco BAM-12 and BAMC-12) works well for sprouting 1 US pound of dried seeds, which can grow to fill the entire container.

Soak dried seeds overnight in water (no sodium bicarbonate), then drain and rinse well. Cover to keep out light while growing. Briefly soak and drain twice per day until sprouts are desired length.

Vegetables Simmer or Stir-Fry With Water

In the past I often steamed vegetables in a stockpot with basket, but now prefer the following two methods with more options for combining various flavors.

Deep Skillet

Add thinly sliced vegetables, spices, and a small amount of water to a deep skillet. Cover with lid and cook at low to medium heat. Periodically move the vegetables with a spatula and add a little more water if needed to prevent burning. The water absorbs the flavors of the food and forms a savory sauce.

Optional: Once the vegetables are done, add leftover precooked grains, legumes, and/or diced raw vegetables and mix together. Cover with lid and allow to warm up.

I use a Lodge 5-quart (12" x 3.4") L10DSK3 cast iron deep skillet, seasoned to protect it from rust, along with a glass lid. Once heated, this heavy (9.3 lb) skillet stays warm for a long time and radiates a lot of heat energy from its surface, far more than stainless steel. Avoid adding a large amount of cold water when this skillet is hot, since the sudden temperature change could cause it to crack. Properly cared for, this skillet will last a lifetime.

Wok

Preheat empty wok for one minute at highest setting on stove. Add finely diced vegetables and briefly cook. Keep the vegetables moving with a wok spatula and periodically add minimal water to prevent sticking. Immediately afterward clean the wok, briefly reheat to evaporate moisture, and rub with minimal coconut oil to prevent rust.

I use a Honey-Can-Do Origins / Joyce Chen 14" flat-bottom wok made of heavy 1.8-mm 15-gauge spun carbon steel for resistance against warping. Mine has remained flat. A mallet could reshape it if needed. The wok came from the Taiwan manufacturer with a clear protective coating, which I removed from the cooking surface by repeatedly scrubbing with fine steel wool and wiping away the powder residue with isopropyl alcohol on tissue paper.

Ascorbic acid is the type of vitamin C that animals internally synthesize, with two extra electrons per molecule to donate to oxidized free radicals and neutralize their harmful effects in the body. Safety

In this section the term vitamin C refers to ascorbic acid, the type that animals internally synthesize. Vitamin C is a nontoxic, water-soluble substance that does not accumulate in the body. Since the 1940s physicians have safely used high-dose oral vitamin C to treat patients. Potential side effects are temporary gastrointestinal disturbances which quickly subside when dosage is reduced.

Observational studies are highly variable in quality of evidence provided, potential bias, and errors. Assuming a cause-and-effect relationship between two variables may be incorrect based on an observed association between them — correlation does not imply causation. Laetrile Quality

Laetrile (with a capital "L") is a refined, concentrated form of natural amygdalin first developed by Ernst T. Krebs Jr. in a patented process. Laetrile turns polarized light in a left-handed direction, and is the form successfully used by the physicians and researchers mentioned above.

Some manufacturers produce iso-amygdalin, an artificial form that may be mislabeled as amygdalin or laetrile (with a lower case "l"). This turns polarized light in both left-handed and right-handed directions, is much less effective against cancer, and can cause adverse reactions in patients.
